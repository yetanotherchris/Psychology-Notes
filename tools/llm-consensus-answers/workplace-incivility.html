Foundational Theoretical Work

Andersson, L. M., & Pearson, C. M. (1999). Tit for tat? The spiraling effect of incivility in the workplace. Academy of Management Review, 24(3), 452-471. https://doi.org/10.5465/amr.1999.2202131

This theoretical paper is the direct precursor to the 2001 empirical study, establishing the conceptual framework for workplace incivility and introducing the "tit-for-tat" spiral model that explains how low-intensity deviant behaviors escalate into serious conflict.

Core Measurement Studies

Cortina, L. M., Magley, V. J., Williams, J. H., & Langhout, R. D. (2001). Incivility in the workplace: Incidence and impact. Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 6(1), 64-80. https://doi.org/10.1037/1076-8998.6.1.64

Published the same year as Pearson et al. (2001), this study developed and validated the Workplace Incivility Scale (WIS), a widely-used measurement instrument. It provides quantitative evidence about the prevalence and psychological effects of workplace incivility, complementing Pearson's qualitative findings.

Blau, G., & Andersson, L. (2005). Testing a measure of instigated workplace incivility. Journal of Occupational and Organizational Psychology, 78(4), 595-614. https://doi.org/10.1348/096317905X26822

This study further developed psychometric instruments for measuring instigated incivility, building directly on the foundational conceptual work.

Impact and Outcomes Research

Lim, S., Cortina, L. M., & Magley, V. J. (2008). Personal and workgroup incivility: Impact on work and health outcomes. Journal of Applied Psychology, 93(1), 95-107. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.93.1.95

This research distinguished between personal (targeted) and workgroup (ambient) incivility, demonstrating their differential impacts on job satisfaction, turnover intentions, psychological distress, and health outcomes.

Porath, C. L., & Erez, A. (2007). Does rudeness really matter? The effects of rudeness on task performance and helpfulness. Academy of Management Journal, 50(5), 1181-1197. https://doi.org/10.5465/amj.2007.20159919

Using experimental methodology, this study demonstrated causal effects of incivility (framed as rudeness) on cognitive performance and prosocial behaviors, showing that even witnessing rudeness significantly reduces task performance.

Porath, C., & Pearson, C. (2013). The price of incivility. Harvard Business Review, 91(1-2), 114-121.

This article quantifies the organizational costs of incivility, providing practical insights into its business impact.

Comprehensive Reviews and Syntheses

Schilpzand, P., De Pater, I. E., & Erez, A. (2016). Workplace incivility: A review of the literature and agenda for future research. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 37(S1), S57-S88. https://doi.org/10.1002/job.1976

This comprehensive review synthesizes two decades of research on workplace incivility, summarizing known antecedents (personality, leadership) and consequences (individual and team-level outcomes), and validates the lasting impact of Pearson et al.'s (2001) framework.

Related Constructs on the Workplace Mistreatment Spectrum

Tepper, B. J. (2000). Consequences of abusive supervision. Academy of Management Journal, 43(2), 178-190. https://doi.org/10.2307/1556375

This highly-cited work examines abusive supervision, a more severe and intentional form of negative interpersonal treatment from supervisors, representing a related but more intense phenomenon on the workplace mistreatment spectrum.

Duffy, M. K., Ganster, D. C., & Pagon, M. (2002). Social undermining in the workplace. Academy of Management Journal, 45(2), 331-351. https://doi.org/10.2307/3069350

This study explores social undermining, examining behaviors intended to hinder others' goal attainment, a related construct often studied alongside incivility.

Aquino, K., & Thau, S. (2009). Workplace victimization: Aggression from the target's perspective. Annual Review of Psychology, 60, 717-741. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.psych.60.110707.163703

This review examines workplace victimization broadly, providing context for understanding incivility within the larger framework of workplace aggression.

Hershcovis, M. S. (2011). "Incivility, social undermining, bullying... oh my!": A call to reconcile constructs within workplace aggression research. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 32(3), 499-519. https://doi.org/10.1002/job.689

This integrative review attempts to distinguish incivility from related constructs like bullying and aggression, providing conceptual clarity.

Application and Intervention Studies

Leiter, M. P., Laschinger, H. K. S., Day, A., & Oore, D. G. (2011). The impact of civility interventions on employee social behavior, distress, and attitudes. Journal of Applied Psychology, 96(6), 1258-1274. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0024442

This study examines practical interventions to reduce incivility and improve workplace civility.

Penney, L. M., & Spector, P. E. (2005). Job stress, incivility, and counterproductive work behavior (CWB): The moderating role of negative affectivity. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(7), 777-796. https://doi.org/10.1002/job.336

This research explores how incivility relates to counterproductive work behaviors, examining the role of individual differences.

Cortina, L. M., & Magley, V. J. (2009). Patterns and profiles of response to incivility in the workplace. Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 14(3), 272-288. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0014934

This study examines how individuals respond to workplace incivility, identifying different response patterns and their consequences.

Miner-Rubino, K., & Cortina, L. M. (2007). Beyond targets: Consequences of vicarious exposure to misogyny at work. Journal of Applied Psychology, 92(5), 1254-1269. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.92.5.1254
