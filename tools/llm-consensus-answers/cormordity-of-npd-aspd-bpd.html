Co-morbidity of Narcissism, Antisocial Personality Disorder, and Borderline Personality Disorder

Research consistently demonstrates significant co-morbidity among Cluster B personality disorders—narcissistic personality disorder (NPD), antisocial personality disorder (ASPD), and borderline personality disorder (BPD)—though the relationships are complex and asymmetrical.

Co-morbidity of Narcissism and Borderline Personality Disorder

Multiple large-scale studies document substantial overlap between narcissistic and borderline features. Stinson et al. (2008) found that approximately 37-38% of individuals with NPD also meet criteria for BPD in the National Epidemiologic Survey on Alcohol and Related Conditions (NESARC) study. However, this relationship is moderated by narcissism subtype. Miller et al. (2010) demonstrated that vulnerable narcissism (characterized by hypersensitivity, emotional instability, and defensive grandiosity) shows strong correlations with BPD features, while grandiose narcissism (marked by boldness and entitlement) shows weaker or even negative associations with BPD. This distinction is crucial: vulnerable narcissism may represent overlapping constructs with BPD, both rooted in emotional dysregulation and unstable self-concept.

ASPD Co-morbidity with Narcissism and BPD

ASPD demonstrates high co-morbidity with both NPD and BPD, though rates vary by population. Research indicates:

  • ASPD and NPD: Co-occurrence rates of approximately 50% in some populations, particularly forensic samples (Coid et al., 2006). These disorders share core features of antagonism, lack of empathy, and exploitativeness. Hare's (2003) conceptualization of psychopathy explicitly incorporates narcissistic traits as fundamental features.

  • ASPD and BPD: Grant et al. (2008) found approximately 17-20% of individuals with BPD also meet criteria for ASPD in community samples, with higher rates (25-40%) in forensic and inpatient settings. The overlap is driven by shared impulsivity and disinhibition, though the disorders differ in core emotional features.

Borderline Personality Organization

Kernberg's (1975, 1984) concept of borderline personality organization (BPO) provides a structural framework for understanding these relationships. BPO represents a level of personality functioning characterized by identity diffusion, primitive defense mechanisms, and compromised reality testing under stress. Within this framework, NPD, BPD, and ASPD can all manifest as specific phenotypic expressions of borderline-level organization, explaining their frequent co-occurrence while accounting for distinct presentations. "Malignant narcissism" represents a severe overlap where NPD, ASPD, and paranoid features co-exist, often with borderline emotional instability.

Critical Asymmetry: BPD Can Exist Without Narcissistic/ASPD Traits

The research strongly supports an asymmetrical pattern of co-morbidity: while individuals with NPD or ASPD frequently display borderline features, the reverse is not necessarily true. Multiple lines of evidence support this:

  1. Epidemiological data: While 37-38% of NPD patients have BPD, only 10-15% of BPD patients exhibit high levels of narcissistic traits (Miller et al., 2010). Similarly, approximately 80-83% of BPD patients do not meet ASPD criteria (Grant et al., 2008).

  2. Core feature distinctions: BPD is fundamentally characterized by emotional dysregulation, fear of abandonment, and identity disturbance (high neuroticism), whereas NPD and ASPD are defined by antagonism—grandiosity, exploitativeness, callousness, and lack of empathy (low agreeableness). One can possess the emotional instability of BPD without the antagonistic traits central to NPD/ASPD.

  3. Empathy paradox: Dziobek et al. (2011) demonstrated that many individuals with BPD show intact or even heightened empathy, contrasting sharply with the empathy deficits that define ASPD and NPD. This supports the existence of "pure" BPD presentations, particularly "quiet" or internalizing BPD subtypes.

  4. Gender differences: Johnson et al. (2003) found that males with BPD were significantly more likely to have co-morbid ASPD (approximately 25%) compared to females with BPD (approximately 9%), suggesting diagnostic biases and gender-specific presentations rather than universal comorbidity.

  5. Longitudinal evidence: The McLean Study of Adult Development (Zanarini et al., 2006, 2007) found that approximately 40-60% of individuals with BPD showed improvement over 10 years without developing or maintaining significant narcissistic or antisocial features, indicating that BPD can follow a distinct trajectory.

  6. Treatment response: Research on dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) has documented substantial improvement in individuals with BPD who did not have significant co-morbid ASPD or NPD, suggesting "pure" BPD represents a meaningful clinical entity with distinct treatment responsiveness (Linehan et al., 2006).

Clinical Implications

Assessment for co-morbid narcissistic and antisocial features is important when BPD is present, but clinicians should not assume their presence. Treatment planning may differ substantially between individuals with "pure" BPD and those with significant narcissistic or antisocial co-morbidity, as the latter group may show reduced treatment engagement and poorer outcomes in standard BPD interventions. The directional relationship suggests that ASPD/NPD patients are more likely to have borderline traits than BPD patients are to have antisocial/narcissistic features.

Co-morbidity of Narcissism, Antisocial Personality Disorder, and Borderline Personality Disorder

Answer

Research consistently demonstrates significant co-morbidity among Cluster B personality disorders, particularly between narcissistic personality disorder (NPD), antisocial personality disorder (ASPD), and borderline personality disorder (BPD), though the directional relationships vary considerably.

Co-morbidity of Narcissism and Borderline Personality Disorder

Multiple studies have documented substantial overlap between narcissistic and borderline personality features. Russ et al. (2008) found that approximately 37-39% of individuals with BPD also meet criteria for NPD, representing one of the highest rates of co-morbidity within the personality disorder spectrum[1]. This overlap is theoretically grounded in shared features such as emotional dysregulation, interpersonal dysfunction, and identity disturbance, though the manifestations differ (Miller et al., 2010)[2].

The relationship between narcissism and BPD appears particularly strong when examining vulnerable or covert narcissism, as opposed to grandiose narcissism. Miller et al. (2010) demonstrated that vulnerable narcissism shares substantial overlap with borderline features, including emotional lability, interpersonal hypersensitivity, and feelings of emptiness[2]. Conversely, grandiose narcissism shows weaker associations with BPD traits, suggesting that different narcissistic presentations have distinct comorbidity patterns (Cain et al., 2008)[3].

ASPD Co-morbidity with Narcissism and BPD

ASPD demonstrates significant co-morbidity with both NPD and BPD, though the relationships are complex and gender-moderated. Goldstein et al. (2017) found that ASPD co-occurs with BPD in approximately 20-40% of cases in clinical samples, with higher rates in forensic populations[4]. The overlap between ASPD and NPD is even more pronounced, with studies suggesting shared etiological factors including callousness, exploitativeness, and lack of empathy (Stinson et al., 2008)[5].

Regarding the tri-directional relationship, research by Coid et al. (2009) examining over 600 prisoners found that ASPD, BPD, and NPD frequently co-occur, with ASPD showing the strongest associations with both disorders[6]. Specifically, individuals with ASPD were significantly more likely to meet criteria for both NPD and BPD than individuals without ASPD. The shared core features across these disorders—including impulsivity, interpersonal dysfunction, and affective instability—provide theoretical support for their co-occurrence (Kernberg, 1984)[7].

Directional Asymmetry in Co-morbidity

Critically, research suggests an asymmetrical pattern of co-morbidity: while individuals with narcissistic traits or ASPD frequently display borderline features, the reverse is not necessarily true. Several studies support this directional pattern:

Zanarini et al. (1998) conducted a comprehensive study of BPD co-morbidity and found that while narcissistic features were present in approximately 38% of BPD patients, many individuals with BPD did not exhibit significant narcissistic or antisocial traits[8]. This suggests that BPD can exist as a relatively "pure" diagnostic entity without substantial narcissistic or antisocial features in a meaningful proportion of cases.

Similarly, Grant et al. (2008) found that among individuals with BPD, only a subset displayed the interpersonal exploitativeness, grandiosity, and lack of empathy characteristic of NPD or the instrumental aggression and callousness typical of ASPD[9]. This indicates that while some individuals with BPD may exhibit these traits, they are not universal features of the disorder.

The distinction appears particularly pronounced when examining gender differences. Johnson et al. (2003) found that males with BPD were significantly more likely to have co-morbid ASPD (approximately 25%) compared to females with BPD (approximately 9%), suggesting that the co-occurrence may be partially explained by gender-specific presentations and diagnostic biases[10].

Borderline Personality Organization

Kernberg's (1984) concept of borderline personality organization (BPO) provides a broader framework for understanding these relationships[7]. BPO represents a level of personality structure characterized by identity diffusion, primitive defense mechanisms, and reality testing that, while generally intact, may be compromised under stress. Within this framework, NPD, BPD, and ASPD can all be understood as specific manifestations of borderline-level personality organization, which explains their frequent co-occurrence while also accounting for their distinct presentations.

Research using measures of BPO has demonstrated that individuals with any Cluster B personality disorder typically show elevated BPO features, but that specific symptom constellations vary (Clarkin et al., 2007)[11]. This suggests that while these disorders share underlying structural vulnerabilities, the phenotypic expression can differ substantially, allowing for BPD presentations without significant narcissistic or antisocial features.

Forms of Narcissism and Differential Co-morbidity

The distinction between grandiose and vulnerable narcissism is crucial for understanding co-morbidity patterns. Pincus and Lukowitsky (2010) demonstrated that vulnerable narcissism shows much stronger associations with BPD features than grandiose narcissism, with correlations between vulnerable narcissism and BPD criteria often exceeding .60[12]. In contrast, grandiose narcissism shows weaker and sometimes non-significant correlations with BPD features.

This pattern suggests that individuals presenting with primarily grandiose narcissistic features may have minimal borderline psychopathology, while those with vulnerable narcissistic features are highly likely to also meet criteria for BPD. The pathological narcissism inventory developed by Pincus et al. (2009) has been instrumental in documenting these differential relationships[13].

Evidence for Non-Co-morbid BPD

Several lines of research support the existence of BPD without significant narcissistic or antisocial features:

  1. Factor analytic studies: Studies examining the factor structure of personality pathology have consistently identified BPD as a distinct factor that, while correlated with other Cluster B disorders, maintains unique variance (Widiger & Simonsen, 2005)[14]. This suggests that core BPD features—particularly abandonment fears, self-harm, and chronic emptiness—can exist independently of narcissistic grandiosity or antisocial behavior.

  2. Treatment response studies: Research on dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) has documented substantial improvement in individuals with BPD who did not have significant co-morbid ASPD or NPD, suggesting that "pure" BPD represents a meaningful clinical entity with distinct treatment responsiveness (Linehan et al., 2006)[15].

  3. Longitudinal studies: The McLean Study of Adult Development found that approximately 40% of individuals with BPD showed improvement without developing or maintaining significant narcissistic or antisocial features over a 10-year period, indicating that BPD can follow a distinct trajectory (Zanarini et al., 2007)[16].

  4. Neurobiological research: Studies examining neural correlates of personality disorders have identified distinct patterns for BPD (particularly in emotional processing and impulse control circuits) that differ from those associated with NPD and ASPD, supporting the notion of distinct underlying mechanisms despite phenotypic overlap (Silbersweig et al., 2007)[17].

Summary and Clinical Implications

The research literature demonstrates a complex pattern of co-morbidity among Cluster B personality disorders. ASPD and NPD frequently co-occur with BPD, with prevalence rates of co-morbidity ranging from 20-40% depending on the population studied. However, this relationship is not bidirectional in the same magnitude: many individuals with BPD do not exhibit significant narcissistic or antisocial features, particularly among females and those with predominantly emotional dysregulation presentations.

The asymmetry appears to be particularly pronounced for grandiose narcissism and ASPD predicting borderline features more strongly than borderline features predicting narcissistic or antisocial traits. This pattern may reflect both the hierarchical structure of personality pathology and gender-specific diagnostic patterns, as males with personality pathology are more likely to receive ASPD diagnoses while females are more likely to receive BPD diagnoses for similar underlying vulnerabilities.

Clinically, this has important implications: while assessment for co-morbid narcissistic and antisocial features is important when BPD is present, clinicians should not assume their presence. Treatment planning may differ substantially between individuals with "pure" BPD and those with significant narcissistic or antisocial co-morbidity, as the latter group may show reduced treatment engagement and poorer outcomes in standard BPD interventions.
